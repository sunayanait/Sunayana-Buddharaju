Wave Function & Schrödinger Equation
Understanding the wave function ψ, wave-particle duality, and the 1D potential well.

What is a Wave Function?

In quantum mechanics, a particle is described not by a definite position, but by a wave function ψ(x, t). Think of it like a ripple on water — spread out in space — that encodes all possible information about a particle.

💡

The wave function itself is not directly measurable. What's physical is |ψ(x,t)|² — the probability density of finding the particle at position x at time t.

The Born Interpretation states:

Probability Density
\[ P(x,t) = |\psi(x,t)|^2 \]
\[ \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} |\psi(x,t)|^2 \, dx = 1 \quad \text{(Normalization)} \]

Normalization ensures the total probability of finding the particle somewhere in space equals 1 — a certainty.

Wave-Particle Duality

One of quantum mechanics' most surprising features: particles like electrons and photons behave as both waves and particles depending on how you observe them.

🔵 Particle Behavior

Detected as a single point on a screen. Carries discrete energy (photon: E = hf). Scatters like a billiard ball.

🌊 Wave Behavior

Creates interference and diffraction patterns (Young's double-slit). Spreads out in space. Has wavelength λ.

de Broglie's relation connects both natures — every particle has an associated wavelength:

de Broglie Wavelength
\[ \lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{mv} \]
where \(h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34}\) J·s (Planck's constant), \(p = mv\) is momentum.

The Schrödinger Equation

The time-dependent Schrödinger equation governs how the wave function evolves in time — it is the fundamental equation of quantum mechanics:

Time-Dependent Schrödinger Equation
\[ i\hbar \frac{\partial \psi(x,t)}{\partial t} = \hat{H}\,\psi(x,t) \] where \(\hat{H} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2} + V(x)\) is the Hamiltonian operator.

For problems where energy is constant (time-independent), we get the simpler time-independent Schrödinger equation:

Time-Independent Schrödinger Equation
\[ \hat{H}\,\psi(x) = E\,\psi(x) \] \[ -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} + V(x)\,\psi = E\,\psi \]

This is an eigenvalue equation: the solutions ψ are eigenfunctions and E are the corresponding eigenvalues (allowed energy levels).

1D Infinite Potential Well (Particle in a Box)

The simplest quantum system: a particle confined in a box of length L with infinitely high walls. Inside: V = 0. Outside: V = ∞ (particle cannot escape).

Eigenfunctions (allowed wave functions)
\[ \psi_n(x) = \sqrt{\frac{2}{L}}\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right), \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots \]
Eigenvalues (quantized energy levels)
\[ E_n = \frac{n^2 \pi^2 \hbar^2}{2mL^2} = \frac{n^2 h^2}{8mL^2} \]
Key insight: energy is quantized — only discrete values E₁, E₂, E₃... are allowed!
🔑

Quantum Relevance: Quantized energy levels are the foundation of quantum computing. A qubit exploits two discrete quantum states (like n=1 and n=2) to store and process information.

Why Quantum Computing?
Classical vs. Quantum computing, and key applications: Decoherence, Cryptography & Optimization.

Classical vs. Quantum Computing

Classical computers manipulate bits — each is either 0 or 1. Quantum computers manipulate qubits, which can exist in superpositions of 0 and 1 simultaneously, enabling an entirely new paradigm.

AspectClassical ComputingQuantum Computing
Basic unitBit (0 or 1)Qubit (superposition of 0 and 1)
ProcessingSequential / parallel circuitsQuantum parallelism via superposition
OperationsLogic gates (AND, OR, NOT)Quantum gates (unitary matrices)
InterferenceNot applicableUses interference to amplify answers
Error handlingReliable, stableSensitive to noise (decoherence)
Best forGeneral computation, databasesOptimization, cryptography, simulation

⚠️ Decoherence — The Big Challenge

Decoherence is the process by which a quantum system loses its quantum properties (superposition, entanglement) due to unwanted interaction with the environment. It is the primary obstacle to building large-scale quantum computers.

🌡️

Qubits must be kept near absolute zero (~0.015 Kelvin) and isolated from vibrations, electromagnetic fields, and even stray photons — any interaction collapses the delicate quantum state.

Decoherence time (T₂) is the characteristic time a qubit maintains its quantum state. Modern superconducting qubits achieve T₂ ~ 100–500 microseconds. Quantum error correction is used to fight decoherence.

🔐 Cryptography

Quantum computers threaten current encryption (RSA, ECC) by efficiently factoring large numbers using Shor's Algorithm. Conversely, quantum mechanics enables Quantum Key Distribution (QKD) — provably secure communication based on the laws of physics.

⚡ Quantum Threat

Shor's algorithm can factor an N-bit number in polynomial time O((log N)³), breaking RSA encryption exponentially faster than classical methods.

🛡️ Quantum Security

QKD (BB84 protocol) uses quantum states to distribute keys. Any eavesdropping attempt disturbs the quantum states and is detectable — absolute security.

📈 Optimization

Grover's Algorithm provides a quadratic speedup for searching unsorted databases — classical: O(N) steps, quantum: O(√N) steps. Quantum annealing and QAOA (Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm) tackle combinatorial problems like logistics, drug discovery, and financial modeling.

Grover's Speedup
Classical search: \(O(N)\)  →  Quantum search: \(O(\sqrt{N})\)

For N = 1,000,000 items: Classical needs 1,000,000 steps; Quantum needs only ~1,000 steps!

Qubits vs. Classical Bits
Definition, representation, and comparison between classical bits and quantum bits (qubits).

The Classical Bit

A classical bit is the fundamental unit of classical information. It has exactly two states: 0 or 1. Physically, it can be a voltage level (low/high), a magnetic domain (north/south), or a capacitor charge (empty/full).

🔲

At any given moment, a bit is definitively either 0 or 1. There is no ambiguity — it's like a light switch: on or off.

The Quantum Bit (Qubit)

A qubit is the fundamental unit of quantum information. Unlike a classical bit, it can exist in a superposition of |0⟩ and |1⟩ simultaneously.

General Qubit State (Superposition)
\[ |\psi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle \] where \(\alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{C}\) (complex numbers) and the normalization condition holds: \[ |\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1 \] \(|\alpha|^2\) = probability of measuring 0, \(\quad |\beta|^2\) = probability of measuring 1.

Physical Implementations of Qubits

TechnologyPhysical Qubit|0⟩ / |1⟩ States
SuperconductingJosephson junction circuitGround / excited energy level
Trapped IonIndividual ions (⁴⁰Ca⁺, ¹⁷¹Yb⁺)Two electronic states
PhotonicSingle photonHorizontal / vertical polarization
Spin QubitElectron or nuclear spinSpin-up ↑ / Spin-down ↓

Superposition & Measurement

Before measurement, a qubit exists in superposition. The act of measurement collapses the superposition to a definite state (0 or 1) with probability determined by α and β:

Example: Equal Superposition
\[ |\psi\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}|0\rangle + \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}|1\rangle \] Here \(|\alpha|^2 = |\beta|^2 = \frac{1}{2}\), so measuring gives 0 or 1 each with 50% probability.

This state (equal superposition) is created by the Hadamard gate H applied to |0⟩ — a core operation in quantum algorithms.

Mathematical Preliminaries
Bra-ket notation, matrix representation, inner & outer products, and tensor products.

Bra-Ket (Dirac) Notation

Invented by Paul Dirac, bra-ket notation is the standard language for quantum states. It is elegant, concise, and compatible with linear algebra.

Ket — Column Vector (State)
\[ |0\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad |1\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \]
Bra — Row Vector (Conjugate Transpose of Ket)
\[ \langle 0| = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \langle 1| = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \]

A general qubit state as a column vector:

\[ |\psi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} \alpha \\ \beta \end{pmatrix} \]

Inner Product (Bra-Ket)

The inner product ⟨φ|ψ⟩ is a scalar (complex number). It measures the "overlap" between two quantum states and is the quantum analogue of dot product.

Inner Product
\[ \langle\phi|\psi\rangle = \sum_i \phi_i^* \psi_i \]
Orthonormality of basis states: \[ \langle 0|0\rangle = 1, \quad \langle 1|1\rangle = 1, \quad \langle 0|1\rangle = 0, \quad \langle 1|0\rangle = 0 \]

The normalization condition for |ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩ expressed via inner product:

\[ \langle\psi|\psi\rangle = |\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1 \]

Outer Product

The outer product |ψ⟩⟨φ| produces a matrix (operator), not a scalar. It maps one quantum state to another.

Outer Products of Basis States
\[ |0\rangle\langle 0| = \begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1&0\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&0\end{pmatrix} \]
\[ |1\rangle\langle 1| = \begin{pmatrix}0\\1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}0&1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}0&0\\0&1\end{pmatrix} \]
Completeness relation: \(\quad |0\rangle\langle 0| + |1\rangle\langle 1| = I\) (identity matrix)

Matrix Representation of Quantum States & Gates

Quantum gates are unitary matrices (U†U = I). They act on qubit states by matrix multiplication:

Identity & Pauli Matrices
\[ I = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&1\end{pmatrix}, \quad X = \begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}, \quad Z = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&-1\end{pmatrix} \]

Example: The X gate (quantum NOT) applied to |0⟩:

\[ X|0\rangle = \begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}0\\1\end{pmatrix} = |1\rangle \quad ✓ \]

Tensor Product

The tensor product ⊗ combines two quantum systems into one larger system. For two qubits, it produces a 4-dimensional state space.

Tensor Product of Two Qubits
\[ |0\rangle \otimes |1\rangle = |01\rangle = \begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}\otimes\begin{pmatrix}0\\1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1\cdot\begin{pmatrix}0\\1\end{pmatrix}\\0\cdot\begin{pmatrix}0\\1\end{pmatrix}\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}0\\1\\0\\0\end{pmatrix} \]
General Two-Qubit State Space
The four basis states of a 2-qubit system: \[ |00\rangle = \begin{pmatrix}1\\0\\0\\0\end{pmatrix},\; |01\rangle = \begin{pmatrix}0\\1\\0\\0\end{pmatrix},\; |10\rangle = \begin{pmatrix}0\\0\\1\\0\end{pmatrix},\; |11\rangle = \begin{pmatrix}0\\0\\0\\1\end{pmatrix} \] For n qubits: state space has dimension \(2^n\).
💡

The power of quantum computing comes directly from tensor products: 300 qubits in superposition can represent more states simultaneously than there are atoms in the observable universe (2³⁰⁰ ≈ 10⁹⁰)!
